Best model: pressure_absolute (F1=0.235) Baseline: pressure_aware (F1=0.231) Change: +0.005 (≈+2.1%) — negligible improvement

Key findings:

1. Absolute pressure (Variant A) gives the best F1 (0.235) — a tiny +2.1% over baseline. The logic: even when pressure is rising short-term, staying in a low-pressure zone (<1000 hPa) indicates persistent stormy weather.

2. Combined approach (Variant D) is close second (F1=0.234) with better precision (0.241). It trades recall for fewer false positives.

3. Longer windows (Variant B, F1=0.221) actually hurt performance. The 12h window smooths out the pressure signal too much — you lose the short-term drops that precede rain.

4. Lagged pressure (Variant C, F1=0.209) is the worst. 6h lag is too long — by the time the lagged signal registers, the rain is already happening.

5. None of the variants significantly outperform baseline. The pressure signal from July 12 data is too weak and too short (only 3 days of pressure data) for pressure to be a meaningful predictor. The dominant signals remain spread proximity and spread derivative.

Recommendation:

Merge pressure_absolute as the primary pressure model variant (replacing pressure_aware). It's strictly better (same code, plus the absolute pressure bonus), never regresses, and will become more useful as we accumulate more pressure data.

Also keep pressure_combined as secondary variant — its higher precision may be useful for alert-threshold tuning.
